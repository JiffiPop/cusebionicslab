Research

In May of 2020, I began working for my Control Systems professor in Syracuse University's Bionics and Control Systems Laboratory with six other students, two of whom were working to obtain a PhD.  At the time, the laboratory was brand new and research had only been conducted for a couple of months.  It was a very exciting time to be working in the lab largely due to the latest and greatest technology for model-based human motion tracking, 3D sculpting, and powerful motors that laid right in front of me.

The premise of this research lab was to develop a system where paraplegic patients could initially exercise their muscles after being bedridden or using a wheelchair for a significant amount of time.  This could potentially allow patients to gain the strength to walk after multiple iterations of this assisted exercise by exploiting the muscles they already have.  This is also referred to as gait rehabilitation.

There are two main steps for this process.  The first step is to utilize the stationary bike.  The bike itself its a tricycle featuring a motor connected into the drivechain system, and cloth electrodes that are placed on the patient's thighs.  In short, the way this works is the electrodes involuntarily contract the patient's muscles and force them to pedal.  This works in conjunction with the motor to assist the pedalling motion as the electrodes alone are not enough to do so.  Exercising with the bike for multiple sessions can build enough muscle to the point where the patient can walk utilizing the lower-limb exoskeleton suit.
